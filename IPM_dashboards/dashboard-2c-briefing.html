Act 1 · Who holds the trade

The wheel: money and asymmetry in one ring

Start with the partners. Every ribbon is a two-way trade relationship — its width is money, its color is who needs whom more. A thick ribbon isn't automatically a risk; a thick, one-sided one is. In the synthetic 2024 panel Russia and China carry the widest inbound ribbons (~15% and ~14% of imports).

Try: click a partner to open its 30-year dyad profile, then use the asymmetry screener to list the one-way dependences above a ratio threshold.

The wheel abstracts geography away — every partner sits at the same distance. Put the same ribbons back on the map and they become corridors with chokepoints. ↓
Act 2 · Where it flows

The atlas: corridors on real geography

The same relationships as routes: corridor width is money, node color is risk, dash drift is direction. Geography adds what the wheel can't say — which dependences share a neighborhood, a strait, a border.

Try: the corridor screener ranks routes by share of trade, risk contribution, and largest cargo.

Wheel and map are both snapshots. The real story of the panel is thirty years of motion — sector by sector, the dependence changed hands. ↓
Act 3 · When it changed hands

The long rotation, sixteen sectors at a time

Each panel is one sector: a risk-contribution line with an ownership strip underneath showing which partner dominates it. Where a strip changes color, the dependence changed hands — Germany's 1995 export monoculture (~55% of export risk) unwinds while China's weight on the import side rises roughly tenfold across the panel, and Russia bumps up after 2022.

Try: the rotation screener lists every handover — filter it to “rotated east”; the risk-shift screener ranks sectors by how much their contribution moved.
