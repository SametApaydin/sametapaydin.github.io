How the world visualizes bilateral data

A survey of fifteen well-known interactive projects that visualize dyadic data — trade, migration, energy, influence — cataloged by visualization paradigm, with notes on what each contributes to a Türkiye trade-dependence (HHI) dashboard.

Prepared July 2026 · companion to the three interactive prototypes built on synthetic dependency data

1Relational paradigm — the dyad as a drawn connection

These projects treat each country-pair as a visible object: a ribbon, an arc, a line. Their strength is who-to-whom at a glance; their cost is clutter past ~30 entities, which every good example solves by aggressive grouping (regions) or top-N filtering.

Global Migration Flows — circular plot

Guy Abel, Nicola Sander, Ramon Bauer · Vienna Institute of Demography / Wittgenstein Centre · featured in Science (2014) and The Economist
Chord diagramDirectional ribbonsTime periods

The most cited bilateral-flow visualization in academia. World regions sit on a circle; migration between each pair is a ribbon whose width at each end encodes directional volume. Direction is read from a gap convention (the ribbon detaches from its origin arc). Five-year period stepping turns it into a story of shifting corridors.

Borrow for IPM The arc-with-ribbons form is the single best "one picture of the whole dependency structure" — Türkiye's arc vs. all partner arcs, ribbon width = trade value, and (their innovation) asymmetric ribbon ends for the import/export mirror.

World Bank — bilateral trade chord

World Bank Open Data blog · d3.js
Chord diagramImbalance encoding

One country vs. top partners plus "others." Its clever detail: the connecting band takes the color of the country that imports more, so trade imbalance direction is visible without reading a single number. Hover reveals exports/imports/balance per dyad.

Borrow for IPM Color-the-ribbon-by-dominant-side is a perfect fit for a dependence index: paint each dyad by which side holds the concentration risk.

peoplemovin

Carlo Zapponi (2011) · featured in multiple dataviz books
Slopegraph / flow columns

Two columns — origins left, destinations right — joined by flowing bands. Click a country and only its flows remain, everything else fades. Proof that a bilateral matrix doesn't need a circle or a map: two sorted lists and bands can be clearer, especially on mobile.

Borrow for IPM The click-to-isolate interaction, and the two-column form as the accessible fallback for the chord (same data, screen-reader-friendlier).

The Refugee Project

Hyperakt & Ekene Ijeoma (2014) · UNHCR data
Radial mapTemporal narrative

Refugee flows as radial bursts from each origin country on a world map, with a year scrubber and embedded historical annotations explaining why each spike happened. The gold standard for pairing dyadic data with narrative context.

Borrow for IPM Annotated time scrubbing — your chart pack's "2022: sanctions begin" callouts belong inside the interactive, not only in the PDF.

Observatory of Economic Complexity — rings & profiles

César Hidalgo / Datawheel · MIT Media Lab origin
RingsLinked views

Beyond its treemaps, the OEC's country-pair profile pages chain a dozen chart types off one dyad selection (Türkiye→Germany: what, how much, since when, vs. whom). The lesson is architectural: one selection state, many synchronized views.

Borrow for IPM The dyad-profile page pattern: select Türkiye–China once, and every panel (treemap, time series, rank) re-scopes to that pair.

2Geographic paradigm — the dyad as a path on a map

Flow maps put the connection in physical space. They win when geography is the story (chokepoints, neighborhoods, regions); they lose when it isn't — long thin arrows to distant partners dominate the ink while the analytically important number (concentration) hides in arrow width.

resourcetrade.earth

Chatham House · Applied Works · UN Comtrade-based CHRTD database (200+ countries, 1,350 resources)
Arc flow mapSankeyFaceted filters

The closest existing product to your project: bilateral resource trade with year, resource-category and country filters, curved arcs on a world map plus a sankey breakdown, and a mirrored exporter/importer perspective. Also notable for surfacing its data-cleaning methodology (mirror reconciliation, outlier flagging) — the same BACI-style problems your index faces.

Borrow for IPM Almost everything: the filter grammar (year × category × partner), the map+sankey pairing, and the honesty about reconciliation. This is the reference product for the flow-map prototype.

FlowmapBlue

Ilya Boyandin · deck.gl / Mapbox · used by MTA, Eurocontrol, AECOM
OD flow mapClusteringAnimation

A general-purpose origin-destination renderer: feed it a locations sheet and a flows sheet, get an interactive flow map with automatic clustering at low zoom, directional animation, and time filtering. Its clustering solves the classic flow-map hairball.

Borrow for IPM Cluster-partners-by-region at rest, expand on zoom/click; animate direction subtly (dashes drifting) rather than with arrowheads.

Shipmap.org

Kiln (Duncan Clark & Robin Houston) · UCL Energy Institute · multiple awards
Animated particlesAmbient narrative

A year of global merchant shipping as moving dots with a narrated tour. Not an analysis tool — an argument that trade physically moves, made unforgettable. The Bosphorus and the canals (your chokepoint stories) are visibly the densest pixels on Earth.

Borrow for IPM Restraint version: a light particle drift along Türkiye's top-10 corridors as the flow map's idle state — atmosphere, not analysis.

Electricity Maps

Electricity Maps ApS (ex-Tomorrow) · live grid data
Choropleth + flow arrowsLive dyads

Countries shaded by carbon intensity; cross-border interconnector flows drawn as animated arrows whose direction and size update live. The best example of layering a country attribute (choropleth) under dyadic flows (arrows) without either drowning the other.

Borrow for IPM The two-layer trick: shade partners by dependency index (attribute), draw flows on top (dyads) — exactly the import-HHI map you need.

3Matrix & decomposition paradigm — the dyad as a cell

The matrix is the least glamorous and most analytically honest form: every dyad×sector gets equal space, nothing occludes, and sorting is the analysis. Rare in public dashboards precisely because it rewards expert readers — which is who IPM's audience is.

Lowy Institute — Asia Power Index

Lowy Institute (annual since 2018)
Indicator matrixWeighted indexAdjustable weights

A composite influence index decomposed into 8 resource + 4 influence measures per country, with the killer feature: users can re-weight the index live and watch rankings reshuffle. Total methodological transparency as an interaction, not a PDF appendix.

Borrow for IPM Expose the index formula as controls — let a reader toggle the elasticity term (σ) or the EU-as-one-supplier assumption and watch Türkiye's number move. That's your chart-pack story 13 as an interaction.

Our World in Data — Grapher

Global Change Data Lab · open source
Small multiplesChart/map/table triad

Every OWID chart ships as chart + map + table views of the same data with a country picker and year scrubber — a uniform grammar across thousands of datasets. Their heatmap/matrix views handle entity×year grids; their design system proves consistency beats novelty for institutional publishing.

Borrow for IPM The chart/map/table triad as a contract for every visualization (we already follow it), and their downloadable-data footer on every chart.

OEC / UN Comtrade Labs — bilateral matrices

Datawheel · UN Statistics Division experiments
Adjacency matrixReorderable

Product×partner matrices with sortable rows/columns and cell shading by value or growth. The reorderable matrix (Bertin's idea, 1967) remains the only form where a 50×40 dyadic dataset is fully readable — every other paradigm samples or aggregates it.

Borrow for IPM Sector×partner HHI-contribution cells with click-to-sort on any row/column, plus marginal bar totals — the "concentration fingerprint."

4Statistical-chart paradigm — the dyad as a data point

Sometimes the honest chart is an ordinary one. These projects visualize dyadic or panel data with scatter, treemap, and ranking forms — trading the visible connection for statistical clarity.

Gapminder Tools

Hans Rosling / Gapminder Foundation
Animated scatterTrails

The animated bubble chart that taught the world to watch panel data move. Its underrated features: per-country trails that turn animation into a drawn trajectory, and the play/scrub contract every time-slider since has copied.

Borrow for IPM Trails. Your "risk migrated, not grew" headline is literally a trajectory: plot each partner as (trade share, concentration contribution) and let China's 30-year trail draw the migration.

Atlas of Economic Complexity

Harvard Growth Lab (already replicated in our Trade Explorer)
TreemapProduct spaceRankings

Covered extensively in this project already; listed here for completeness as the origin of the treemap-plus-product-space grammar and the direct ancestor of your chart pack's aesthetics.

Borrow for IPM Already borrowed — see the Trade Explorer prototype.

Bruegel / ECB-style dependency monitors

European think-tank dashboards (Bruegel datasets, ECB dependency papers)
Ranked barsThreshold lists

The policy-shop house style: "products where supplier X exceeds N% of imports" as ranked, filterable bar lists — your chart-pack stories 12 and 14 are this form. Less a visualization innovation than proof that threshold + ranked bars is what policymakers actually cite.

Borrow for IPM A "chokepoint screener" table view: filter dyad×sector cells by share threshold, sort by value — the citable output format.

Design verdict for the three prototypes

A — Dependency Wheel (relational): Türkiye-centered chord with partner arcs, ribbon width = trade value, ribbon color = who-depends-on-whom, import/export mirror toggle, 30-year scrubber with annotated events, click-to-isolate a dyad.

B — Flow Atlas (geographic): tile-free real-geometry world map (simplified coastlines), partners shaded by dependency contribution, curved flows on top, sector filter, both directions, cluster-by-region at rest.

C — Risk Matrix (analytical): sector × partner HHI-contribution heatmap, sortable both axes, marginal totals, threshold screener table, and a live "what counts as one supplier" toggle (EU-27 merged vs. separate) à la Lowy's re-weighting.

All three run on the same synthetic dependency dataset (44 partners × 16 sectors × 30 years × imports/exports, seeded deterministic), so they can be compared fairly — and swapped to the real BACI-derived index without changing the rendering layer.
